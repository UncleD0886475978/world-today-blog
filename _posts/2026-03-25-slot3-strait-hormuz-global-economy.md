**Analysis — March 25, 2026**

Twenty-one miles. At its narrowest point the Strait of Hormuz is just 33 kilometres wide — a sliver of water between Iran and Oman carrying approximately one-fifth of the world's oil and one-third of its liquefied natural gas. For decades this has been the world's most strategically critical chokepoint. Now Iran is using it as a weapon.

## The Declaration That Shook Markets

When Iran's IRGC declared the Strait closed to US-aligned ships, the market reaction was immediate. Brent crude surged above $110 a barrel — the highest since 2022. Wall Street recorded its worst single day since the pandemic. Supply chains running from Gulf oil fields to refineries in Japan, South Korea, China, and India began seizing up.

## Southeast Asia: The Hardest Hit

The Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia are almost entirely dependent on imported oil — and nearly all of it travels through Hormuz. Fuel prices surged. In Manila, transport workers marched near the presidential palace. In Thailand, motorists queued for hours at petrol stations.

## The US Policy Reversal

Economic pressure forced a remarkable reversal: Washington eased sanctions on Russian and Venezuelan crude — freeing Russia from oil export restrictions — driven entirely by the economic cost of the Iran war. This decision represents one of the most dramatic shifts in US energy sanctions policy in decades.

## A Historical Parallel

The last time Hormuz became a battleground was the Tanker War of the 1980s. The US eventually deployed naval escorts. Trump is now leaning on Japan and allies to send warships — a direct modern parallel. The difference: Iran is not fighting a regional war. It is fighting the United States. The stakes are incomparably higher.
