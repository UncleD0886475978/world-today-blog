**United Nations — March 26, 2026**

The UN General Assembly adopted a landmark resolution Wednesday declaring the transatlantic trafficking of enslaved Africans "the gravest crime against humanity" and calling for reparations — the most significant such acknowledgement in more than two decades.

## What the Resolution Says

Backed by African nations and Caribbean states, the resolution formally recognises the slave trade as an unprecedented crime against humanity and calls on former colonial powers to engage in reparations discussions. It does not specify amounts, but establishes a political framework for formal negotiations potentially involving financial transfers, debt cancellation, development assistance, and formal apologies.

## The Reparations Debate

Proponents argue the wealth of former colonial powers was built directly on enslaved labour — a foundation continuing to shape global inequality today. Critics argue determining liability across generations is legally and practically impossible.

The resolution sidesteps these debates by calling for dialogue rather than mandating specific transfers — a compromise allowing broader support but disappointing advocates wanting binding commitments.

## Historical Significance

The 2001 Durban Declaration acknowledged slavery as a crime against humanity but stopped short of reparations language. Wednesday's resolution goes further. For African and Caribbean nations that have campaigned for this recognition for decades, it represents a meaningful step toward justice for history's largest forced migration.
