Geneva, March 28, 2026 – The halls of international diplomacy are once again alive with intense debate, as leaders at the ongoing World Economic Forum special session in Geneva confront a growing chorus of calls for 'economic sovereignty' and 'strategic autonomy.' Discussions, spurred by persistent supply chain vulnerabilities, technological rivalry, and heightened geopolitical friction, signal a potential turning point away from decades of accelerating globalization. While the imperative for resilience and security is undeniable, the rhetoric around decoupling and protectionism carries a distinct and unsettling echo from the pages of history.

## Echoes of the Interwar Years

The current climate bears a striking resemblance to the period between the two World Wars. Following the devastation of World War I, nations initially sought to rebuild through international cooperation. However, the optimism quickly faded, giving way to a dangerous surge of economic nationalism. The infamous Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930 in the United States, designed to protect domestic industries, triggered a cascade of retaliatory tariffs from other nations. This tit-for-tat protectionism choked international trade, exacerbating the Great Depression and fostering an environment of suspicion and hostility. Global trade volumes plummeted, industries collapsed, and unemployment soared, ultimately contributing to the conditions that led to World War II. The lesson was stark: economic isolationism can have devastating consequences, not just for prosperity but for global peace.

## The Modern Imperative for Resilience

Today's push for 'economic sovereignty' stems from a different, yet equally pressing, set of circumstances. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the fragility of highly optimized global supply chains, leading to shortages of critical goods from medical supplies to semiconductors. Subsequent geopolitical events, particularly intensified competition between major powers and regional conflicts, have further highlighted the risks of over-reliance on single sources or adversarial nations. Governments are now actively promoting 'reshoring' – bringing production back home – or 'friend-shoring' – diversifying supply chains to politically aligned nations. The goal is to build resilience, reduce strategic vulnerabilities, and ensure access to essential goods and technologies in times of crisis. These are legitimate concerns, reflecting a necessary re-evaluation of national security in an interconnected world.

## Navigating a Fragmented World

However, the path to resilience is fraught with peril if unchecked. A wholesale retreat from global integration risks sacrificing the efficiencies and innovations that have driven global growth and lifted millions out of poverty. Excessive protectionism could lead to higher prices for consumers, reduced choice, and slower technological advancement. Developing nations, often integrated into global supply chains at lower tiers, could be particularly vulnerable to economic fragmentation, hindering their development prospects. The challenge lies in finding a delicate balance: building robust, diversified, and secure supply chains without collapsing into a zero-sum game of economic nationalism. This requires strategic foresight, careful policy calibration, and, crucially, continued international dialogue and cooperation.

The world stands at a critical juncture. The forces pushing for greater national autonomy are powerful and understandable, yet history offers a potent warning against the dangers of an unbridled retreat from global economic engagement. Leaders in Geneva and beyond must learn from the mistakes of the interwar period, fostering resilience through collaboration rather than isolation. The goal should be a more secure and diversified global economy, not a fragmented one destined to repeat the errors of the past.
