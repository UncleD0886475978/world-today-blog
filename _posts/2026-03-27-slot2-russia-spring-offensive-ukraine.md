**Moscow / Kyiv — March 27, 2026**

Vladimir Putin is watching Tehran with quiet satisfaction — and preparing to act. As Western attention pivots to the Middle East and US weapons flows to Ukraine are disrupted, Russian military planners are finalising preparations for what analysts call the largest coordinated offensive push since the failed 2022 advance on Kyiv.

## The Strategic Window

Russia's calculus is straightforward: the conditions constraining its offensive ambitions — robust Western weapons deliveries, US political attention, NATO solidarity — have been temporarily disrupted by the Iran war. The window may not last long. But it is real.

US weapons are being diverted to Middle East operations. The 82nd Airborne has deployed to the Middle East rather than reinforcing NATO's eastern flank. European allies are scrambling to cover the gap but their own stockpiles are depleted after three years of Ukraine transfers.

## The Front Line

Russian forces have intensified operations across Pokrovsk, Lyman, Kostiantynivka, and Zaporizhzhia simultaneously — probing for weaknesses. Daily combat engagements have risen above 200, straining Ukrainian rotation and logistics. Ukrainian forces remain resilient but the attrition rate is climbing.

## Rubio at the G7

Secretary Rubio travelled to France for a G7 Foreign Ministers meeting attempting to shore up Allied support for the Iran war. European ministers asked directly whether the US remains committed to Ukrainian victory. His answers were described as "reassuring but not convincing."

Meanwhile Hezbollah leaders explicitly rejected any notion of seeking a ceasefire with Israel — emboldened by Iran's military performance and sensing a moment of regional strength.
