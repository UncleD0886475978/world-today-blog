**Lausanne — March 28, 2026**

The International Olympic Committee announced a sweeping new eligibility policy requiring all athletes competing in women's sports categories to undergo a one-time DNA test — a cheek swab or blood test screening for male chromosomes. The policy represents the most significant change to Olympic eligibility rules in decades.

## The Science

IOC President Kirsty Coventry said the policy is grounded in scientific evidence on performance advantages conferred by male chromosomes in strength, power, and endurance sports. "At the Olympic Games, even the smallest margins can be the difference between victory and defeat," Coventry said. "It's absolutely clear that it would not be fair for biological males to compete in the female category."

## A Striking Reversal

As recently as 2024, the IOC framed inclusion as a core value and deferred to individual sports federations. That policy produced inconsistent rules and a controversial boxing match at the Paris Olympics that generated widespread debate. President Trump's February 2025 executive order calling for clarity in women's sports accelerated political pressure on the IOC to establish a universal standard.

## Divided Reactions

Sports integrity advocates welcomed what they called long-overdue protection of fair competition. LGBTQ+ advocacy organisations condemned the policy as discriminatory, arguing it targets transgender athletes with stigmatising medical requirements beyond what science requires.

The policy applies to all sports at the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics. Athletes testing positive for male chromosomes will be ineligible for women's categories — they may compete in open categories where they exist.
