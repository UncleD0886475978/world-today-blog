Washington D.C. – March 12, 2026

The global economic landscape in early 2026 is marked by a palpable shift towards national self-reliance, particularly in sectors deemed critical for security and future prosperity. From rare earth minerals and advanced semiconductors to pharmaceutical ingredients and renewable energy components, nations are increasingly implementing policies aimed at securing domestic supply chains and limiting foreign dependency. This strategic pivot, often termed 'de-risking' or 'friend-shoring,' reflects a growing anxiety over geopolitical stability and the resilience of globalized supply networks. Yet, as the world erects new economic fences, historical precedents from the early 20th century offer a stark warning about the potential consequences of such fragmentation.

## The Resurgence of Economic Fences
The current trajectory sees major economies deploying a range of tools to bolster their domestic industries and control strategic assets. Export controls on critical technologies, substantial subsidies for local manufacturing, and preferential trade agreements with perceived allies are becoming commonplace. The rationale is clear: recent disruptions, from the COVID-19 pandemic to ongoing regional conflicts and heightened geopolitical competition, have exposed vulnerabilities in a hyper-interconnected world. Leaders argue that national security now extends beyond military might to encompass economic resilience, demanding secure access to vital resources and technological capabilities. This has fueled a drive towards strategic autonomy, prompting nations to re-evaluate the benefits of unfettered globalization against the perceived risks of interdependence.

## A Look Back: Mercantilism to the Great Depression
The impulse to protect national economic interests is not new. From the 17th to the 18th centuries, mercantilism dominated European economic thought, advocating for state control over trade to maximize exports and accumulate wealth, often through colonial expansion and tariffs. However, a more direct parallel to today's climate can be found in the interwar period following World War I. The decade after the Great War saw a retreat from international cooperation and a surge in economic nationalism. Nations, grappling with war debts, reparations, and the onset of the Great Depression, increasingly turned inwards. The infamous Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930 in the United States, which significantly raised import duties, triggered a wave of retaliatory tariffs from other countries. Global trade plummeted by an estimated two-thirds between 1929 and 1934, deepening the economic crisis worldwide. This era was characterized by a scramble for self-sufficiency (autarky), with nations attempting to produce everything domestically or within their imperial blocs, often at the expense of efficiency and international goodwill. The resulting economic desperation fueled political extremism and heightened international tensions, laying groundwork for future conflicts.

## The Perils of Self-Sufficiency
History teaches us that while the pursuit of national security is legitimate, unchecked economic nationalism carries profound risks. The interwar period demonstrated how protectionist policies can lead to a vicious cycle of retaliation, shrinking global markets, stifling innovation, and ultimately impoverishing nations. Reduced trade means less efficient allocation of resources, higher costs for consumers, and slower economic growth. Furthermore, the economic friction generated by such policies often spills over into the diplomatic sphere, escalating trade disputes into broader geopolitical confrontations. The erosion of trust and the weakening of international institutions, which were painstakingly built after World War II to prevent a recurrence of these failures, are also significant concerns. In an increasingly complex and interconnected world, genuine self-sufficiency for any major nation remains an elusive, if not impossible, goal, and the pursuit of it can inadvertently create new vulnerabilities and foster an environment ripe for misunderstanding and conflict.

As the international community navigates the delicate balance between national resilience and global cooperation, the lessons from the past loom large. While the specific economic and technological contexts of 2026 differ from the 1930s, the fundamental human impulses and policy pitfalls remain strikingly similar. Avoiding a return to the destructive economic rivalries of the interwar years will require a renewed commitment to multilateral dialogue, transparent trade rules, and a recognition that collective security and prosperity are often best achieved through collaboration, not isolation. The challenge lies in forging policies that genuinely enhance national security without inadvertently undermining the very global stability upon which long-term prosperity depends.
