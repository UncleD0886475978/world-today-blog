**Washington — March 27, 2026**

In a landmark ruling with industry-reshaping consequences, a court found Meta and YouTube negligent in the design and operation of addictive platforms that harm users' mental health — the most significant judicial finding against social media companies in history.

## What the Court Found

The ruling determined both companies knowingly designed their platforms with features — infinite scroll, algorithmic amplification, variable reward notifications — that exploit psychological vulnerabilities and create compulsive usage patterns, particularly in young users. The court found this design was intentional, driven by business models that monetise attention regardless of cost to user wellbeing.

The finding of negligence rather than intentional harm means civil liability but not criminal exposure. However, the scale of potential claims — hundreds of millions of users, years of documented harm — could produce damages dwarfing any previous tech settlement.

## The Mental Health Evidence

The ruling drew on a decade of evidence: internal company research showing executives knew their products harmed teenagers, epidemiological data linking social media use to rising adolescent anxiety and depression, and testimony from former employees describing deliberate decisions to prioritise engagement over safety.

## Global Implications

The EU's Digital Services Act already requires platforms to assess mental health risks. This US ruling strengthens the legal framework for similar actions in European jurisdictions. Australia, which passed social media age restrictions in 2024, is expected to cite the ruling in pending legislative debates.

Both Meta and YouTube indicated they would appeal. But the direction of travel — toward greater platform accountability for addictive design — now has judicial authority behind it.
