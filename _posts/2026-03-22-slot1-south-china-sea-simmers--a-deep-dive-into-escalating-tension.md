Singapore, Sunday, March 22, 2026

The waters of the South China Sea, long a crucible of geopolitical competition, are once again at a critical juncture. A recent series of assertive actions by Beijing, culminating in a declared "exclusion zone" around the Second Thomas Shoal (known as Ayungin Shoal in the Philippines) and subsequent confrontations with Philippine resupply missions, has brought the long-simmering dispute back to the forefront of international attention. This latest escalation not only tests the resolve of Manila but also scrutinizes the commitment of its allies and the efficacy of international law in one of the world's most vital maritime arteries.

## The Contested Waters: A Brief History

The South China Sea is claimed in part or whole by China, Vietnam, the Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan. At the heart of many disputes lies China's expansive "nine-dash line" claim, which encompasses roughly 90&#37; of the sea, overlapping with the exclusive economic zones (EEZs) of several ASEAN nations. This claim is largely rejected by international law, most notably by the 2016 ruling of the Permanent Court of Arbitration, which sided with the Philippines and deemed China's historical claims without legal basis under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). Despite this, Beijing has consistently dismissed the ruling, continuing its assertive presence and construction of artificial islands equipped with military facilities. Second Thomas Shoal, where the Philippine Navy deliberately grounded the BRP Sierra Madre in 1999 to assert its sovereignty, remains a potent symbol of Manila's defiance and a constant flashpoint for confrontations.

## Manila's Stand and Beijing's Assertions

Under President Ferdinand Marcos Jr., the Philippines has adopted a more robust stance in asserting its maritime rights, moving away from the more conciliatory approach of his predecessor. Resupply missions to the BRP Sierra Madre have become high-stakes endeavors, frequently met with aggressive maneuvers by Chinese Coast Guard vessels, including water cannon attacks and blocking tactics. The recent declaration by China of a "temporary environmental protection and fisheries management exclusion zone" around Second Thomas Shoal, effectively barring Philippine access, represents a significant escalation. While framed as ecological conservation, critics view it as a thinly veiled attempt to solidify de facto control over the shoal, further encroaching on the Philippines' EEZ and challenging its sovereign presence. Manila has vehemently rejected this unilateral declaration, vowing to continue its lawful operations and protect its territorial integrity.

## The US Role and Regional Dynamics

The escalating tensions place significant pressure on the United States, which maintains a mutual defense treaty with the Philippines. Washington has repeatedly reaffirmed its commitment to the Philippines and to upholding freedom of navigation in the South China Sea, conducting joint patrols and military exercises with Manila. The recent deployment of a US Navy reconnaissance aircraft in proximity to the latest confrontation underscored this commitment, signaling Washington's intent to deter further Chinese aggression. Regionally, ASEAN remains divided, with some members hesitant to directly challenge China due to economic ties, while others express growing alarm over Beijing's actions. Efforts to finalize a Code of Conduct in the South China Sea have made slow progress, highlighting the complexities of achieving consensus amidst competing interests and external pressures. Other Indo-Pacific allies, such as Japan and Australia, have also voiced concerns, emphasizing the importance of a rules-based international order.

## Navigating the Brink: Economic and Security Stakes

Beyond the immediate territorial disputes, the South China Sea holds immense economic and strategic importance. It is a critical conduit for global trade, with an estimated one-third of global maritime shipping, valued at trillions of dollars annually, passing through its waters. The region is also believed to hold significant untapped oil and natural gas reserves, as well as rich fishing grounds vital for regional food security. The ongoing disputes, therefore, carry substantial economic implications, threatening supply chains and energy security. From a security perspective, the risk of miscalculation or accidental collision remains high, with the potential to quickly escalate into a broader regional conflict involving major powers. The militarization of artificial islands further complicates the security landscape, transforming what were once minor features into strategic outposts.

The latest developments at Second Thomas Shoal underscore the persistent fragility of peace in the South China Sea. While the Philippines continues to assert its sovereignty and the United States reaffirms its alliances, China shows no signs of relenting on its expansive claims. The international community watches closely, aware that the resolution—or lack thereof—of these disputes will have profound implications not only for regional stability but also for the future of international law and the global balance of power. The challenge lies in finding a diplomatic off-ramp that respects sovereign rights and international norms, a task that appears increasingly daunting with each new confrontation.
