**Tehran / Islamabad — March 25, 2026**

Iran officially dismissed the United States 15-point ceasefire proposal as "extremely maximalist and unreasonable" — a blunt rejection that punctured hopes for a swift end to the war now in its 26th day. The response came hours after Pakistan confirmed it had delivered US demands to Tehran, acting as primary intermediary in the most significant diplomatic exchange since hostilities began February 28.

## What the 15 Points Demand

Two Pakistani officials described the proposal broadly: sanctions relief conditioned on compliance, complete rollback of Iran's nuclear programme, strict missile limits, immediate reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, and restrictions on Iran's support for armed groups including Hezbollah and the Houthis. An Egyptian mediator confirmed the proposal also restricts Iran's support for regional proxy forces — a demand Tehran views as an existential threat to its strategic deterrence.

## Iran's Response

Foreign Minister Araghchi walked a careful line — denying negotiations while acknowledging Washington transmits messages through intermediaries. "This does not mean negotiations," he said. But crucially he added that Iran wants a "permanent end to the conflict" and is demanding compensation for destruction caused by weeks of US-Israeli strikes.

## Israel Surprised

Israeli officials, pressing Trump to intensify the campaign, were reportedly surprised by the peace push. Tel Aviv fears Washington may freeze the conflict before Iran's capabilities are sufficiently degraded — creating unusual friction in the US-Israel relationship.

## Pakistan: The Unlikely Peacemaker

Pakistan's role reflects remarkable diplomatic positioning — warm relations simultaneously with the Trump administration, Tehran, and Gulf states. Prime Minister Sharif has offered to host formal talks. The rejection is not the end of diplomacy. It is the opening move.
